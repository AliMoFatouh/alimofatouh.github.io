The Sport

What is Archery?

Archery is the practice of shooting arrows with a bow — one of humanity's oldest skills, used for hunting, warfare, and ceremony for over 10,000 years. Today it is a precision Olympic sport and a deeply meditative hobby practiced worldwide.

Recurve Bow

The recurve is the only bow used in the Olympic Games. Its distinctive tips curve away from the archer, storing more energy than a straight bow. Used since ancient times by mounted warriors and refined over millennia, it demands precise posture, consistent anchor points, and excellent breath control. Most beginners and competitive archers start here.

Compound Bow

The compound bow uses a system of cables and cams (pulleys) to reduce the draw weight at full draw — a mechanic known as "let-off." This allows the archer to hold steady for longer and aim with greater precision. Popular in hunting and target shooting, it is a triumph of modern engineering applied to an ancient art.

Traditional Bow

Traditional archery — longbow or bare bow — strips away every mechanical aid. No sights, no stabilisers, no let-off. The archer relies entirely on instinct, muscle memory, and feel built through thousands of repetitions. It is the purest and most demanding form of the sport, and the most historically rich.

My Reasons

Why I Love It

When you draw a bow, the whole world narrows to a single point. There is no room for distraction — your mind, body, and breath must all align in one perfect moment. It is the closest thing to meditation I have ever found in a sport.

Archery is not about strength or speed. It is about repetition, stillness, and consistency. Progress comes slowly and honestly — and that makes every improvement feel genuinely earned.

Your groupings do not lie. Session by session, those scattered arrows tighten into clusters. There is a quiet satisfaction in watching your arrow land exactly where your mind intended it to go.

Every time I nock an arrow, I am doing something humans have done for thousands of years — from ancient hunters to Olympic champions. There is something quietly profound about that continuity.
